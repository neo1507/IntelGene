                                  Apple Inc.


1. Basic Company Information


Apple Inc., established in 1976, represents a rare blend of technological precision and human-centered design philosophy. Founded by Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne, the company quickly distinguished itself by redefining how individuals interact with machines. Headquartered in Cupertino, California, Apple operates as a global technology leader under the leadership of CEO Tim Cook.

The brand identity of Apple is deeply intentional. Its name conveys simplicity and familiarity, contrasting sharply with the complex terminology that dominated early computing. The iconic bitten apple logo carries layered symbolism—suggesting knowledge, curiosity, and a deliberate departure from convention. Over time, the logo’s evolution from a colorful palette to a minimalist monochrome reflects Apple’s broader design philosophy: clarity over complexity.

The company’s guiding ethos, often encapsulated through its “Think Different” positioning, is not merely a slogan but a strategic mindset. It reinforces Apple’s commitment to challenging norms, encouraging creativity, and delivering products that feel both intuitive and transformative.


Apple Inc. is one of the most globally recognized and influential technology companies, known for its innovation, premium products, and strong ecosystem integration. The name “Apple” was chosen to be simple, memorable, and approachable, which contrasts with the complex and technical naming conventions commonly used in the technology industry during its founding period. This simplicity helped the company establish a strong identity early on, making it easier for consumers to connect with the brand. Over time, the name Apple has become synonymous with quality, creativity, and innovation, representing not just a company but a lifestyle and a philosophy of design thinking. The brand has evolved from a small startup focused on personal computers into a multinational corporation offering a wide range of products and services, including smartphones, laptops, tablets, wearable devices, and digital services. The name itself reflects the company’s mission to make technology more human-centered and accessible, emphasizing usability and elegance. Apple’s consistent branding, combined with its strong product ecosystem, has reinforced the value of its name in global markets. Today, the name Apple carries significant weight in financial markets, consumer perception, and technological leadership. It is often associated with premium quality, cutting-edge innovation, and strong customer loyalty. The simplicity of the name also contributes to its universal appeal across cultures and languages, making it one of the most effective brand names in corporate history. Overall, the company name Apple Inc. is not just an identifier but a powerful symbol of technological advancement, design excellence, and global influence.


2. Introduction


Apple operates as a vertically integrated enterprise, controlling both hardware and software ecosystems with exceptional precision. This integration allows the company to deliver seamless user experiences that competitors struggle to replicate. Its influence extends across multiple domains, including personal computing, mobile communication, digital entertainment, and financial technology.

With a presence spanning continents, Apple has established itself not only as a commercial entity but also as a cultural symbol. Its products are recognized for setting benchmarks in design, usability, and performance. The company’s scale is reflected in its global retail footprint, supply chain sophistication, and consistent financial performance.

More importantly, Apple’s relevance lies in its ability to anticipate and shape consumer expectations. It does not merely respond to market demand; it actively defines it, positioning itself as both an innovator and a trendsetter.


3. Founding Story



The foundation of Apple is rooted in a partnership between technical brilliance and visionary thinking. Steve Wozniak engineered the early prototypes, while Steve Jobs identified their broader potential in transforming personal computing. Their collaboration led to the creation of the Apple I, followed by the commercially successful Apple II.

At a time when computing was confined to institutions, Apple’s mission to democratize technology was both ambitious and disruptive. However, internal conflicts and strategic misalignments led to a period of instability, culminating in Jobs’ departure. The company’s trajectory shifted dramatically upon his return in 1997, marking a period of reinvention.

This phase introduced a renewed focus on simplicity, design excellence, and product coherence. It laid the groundwork for innovations that would later redefine entire industries, demonstrating how leadership vision can reshape organizational destiny.



4. Leadership and Management


Apple’s leadership narrative is characterized by two distinct yet complementary styles. Steve Jobs embodied visionary intensity, driving innovation through an uncompromising focus on product excellence. His approach emphasized intuition, design, and the ability to foresee latent consumer needs.

Tim Cook, in contrast, represents operational mastery. His tenure has been defined by scalability, efficiency, and strategic expansion into services and global markets. Under his leadership, Apple has achieved record financial milestones while maintaining its core identity.

The company’s governance structure supports this balance by fostering cross-functional collaboration and maintaining strict confidentiality in product development. This ensures alignment across departments and preserves the integrity of Apple’s innovation pipeline.



5. Business Model


Apple’s business model integrates premium hardware sales with a rapidly expanding services ecosystem. The company generates substantial revenue from flagship products such as the iPhone, while services like iCloud, Apple Music, and Apple Pay contribute to recurring income streams.

A defining feature of this model is ecosystem lock-in. Each product enhances the value of others, creating a network effect that strengthens customer retention. This approach not only drives sales but also increases lifetime customer value.

Rather than competing on price, Apple positions itself as a provider of superior experience. This premium strategy enables high margins and reinforces brand perception, making its products aspirational yet functional.



6. Products and Services



Apple’s product portfolio is intentionally curated to ensure coherence and quality. Core offerings include the iPhone, Mac, iPad, Apple Watch, and AirPods. Each product is engineered to integrate seamlessly with others, creating a unified ecosystem.

The services segment extends this ecosystem into the digital domain. Platforms such as Apple Music, Apple TV+, and iCloud provide continuous engagement, transforming Apple from a product company into a service-oriented platform.

What distinguishes Apple’s offerings is not just functionality but experience. Every interaction, from unboxing to daily usage, is designed to feel intuitive and refined.


7. Growth Journey and Timeline


Apple’s evolution can be understood through phases of innovation, decline, and resurgence. The introduction of the Macintosh established early credibility, while subsequent challenges tested its resilience.

The late 1990s marked a turning point, with the launch of products that redefined Apple’s identity. The iPod revolutionized music consumption, and the iPhone reimagined mobile communication, positioning Apple as a leader in multiple industries.

In recent years, diversification into services and wearables has ensured sustained growth, reducing dependence on any single product category.



8. Financial Overview



Apple’s financial architecture is built on high-margin products and disciplined cost management. Its revenue streams are diversified, with services providing stability against hardware cycles.
The company’s valuation reflects investor confidence in its long-term strategy. Strong cash reserves enable continuous investment in innovation, acquisitions, and shareholder returns.

it has built a highly resilient and sophisticated financial structure that reflects both strategic planning and operational discipline. The company generates revenue through a balanced mix of hardware products such as the iPhone, Mac, iPad, and wearables, along with a rapidly growing services segment that includes the App Store, iCloud, Apple Music, and other subscription-based offerings. This diversification plays a crucial role in stabilizing income, especially during periods when hardware sales may fluctuate due to market saturation or economic conditions. Apple’s premium pricing strategy allows it to maintain consistently high gross margins compared to competitors, while its strong brand equity gives it the power to sustain these prices without significantly affecting demand. The company’s ability to generate substantial operating cash flow enables continuous investment in research and development, ensuring that innovation remains at the core of its growth strategy. Additionally, Apple maintains one of the largest cash reserves in the corporate world, which provides financial security and flexibility for acquisitions, technological advancements, and shareholder returns such as dividends and share buybacks. Its disciplined cost management, efficient global supply chain, and strategic procurement practices further enhance profitability. Apple also adheres to strict financial reporting standards and transparency, reinforcing investor confidence and contributing to its high market valuation. Overall, its financial model is not only profitable but also sustainable, positioning the company for long-term success in a dynamic global market.

This model has proven effective in supporting Apple’s long-term goals, enabling it to consistently deliver innovative products while maintaining operational efficiency and strategic focus.

25. How Company Started




Apple Inc. began its journey in 1976 when Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak collaborated to create a personal computer that could be used by individuals rather than large organizations. The company started in a garage, which has since become a symbol of innovation and entrepreneurial spirit. Their first product, the Apple I, was a simple computer designed and built by Steve Wozniak, while Steve Jobs focused on marketing and business strategy. The Apple I was sold as a kit, requiring users to assemble parts themselves, but it marked the beginning of a new era in personal computing. The founders aimed to make technology more accessible and user-friendly, which was a revolutionary idea at the time. The success of the Apple I led to the development of the Apple II, which became one of the first mass-produced personal computers and achieved significant commercial success. This early success helped establish Apple as a key player in the emerging computer industry. The company quickly gained attention for its innovative approach and focus on design and usability. Despite facing challenges such as limited resources and competition, Apple continued to grow and expand its product offerings. The early years of the company were characterized by experimentation, risk-taking, and a strong belief in the potential of personal computing. Steve Jobs’ vision of combining technology with design played a crucial role in shaping the company’s direction. The garage startup story has become a defining part of Apple’s identity, symbolizing creativity and determination. Overall, the way Apple started reflects its core values of innovation, simplicity, and user-focused design.



26. Important Milestone

One of the earliest milestones was the launch of the Apple II, which became one of the first successful personal computers and established Apple as a major player in the market. Another key milestone was the introduction of the Macintosh in 1984, which popularized the graphical user interface and mouse, making computers more accessible to the general public. The return of Steve Jobs in the late 1990s marked a turning point for the company, leading to the introduction of innovative products such as the iMac, which revitalized Apple’s brand and financial performance. The launch of the iPod in 2001 revolutionized the music industry, followed by the iTunes Store, which changed how digital content was distributed. One of the most significant milestones was the introduction of the iPhone in 2007, which transformed the smartphone industry and redefined mobile technology. The iPad, launched in 2010, created a new category of devices, further expanding Apple’s product ecosystem. The development of the App Store also played a crucial role in building a global platform for developers and users. More recently, the transition to Apple Silicon has been a major technological milestone, improving performance and efficiency in its devices. Each of these milestones reflects Apple’s ability to innovate and adapt to changing market conditions. They also demonstrate the company’s focus on creating products that redefine industries rather than simply competing within them. Overall, Apple’s milestones highlight its role as a pioneer in technology and its continuous pursuit of innovation

36. apple has three leadership characteristics-




Apple Inc. Ever since Steve Jobs implemented the functional organization, Apple’s managers at every level, from senior vice president on down, have been expected to possess three key leadership characteristics: deep expertise that allows them to meaningfully engage in all the work being done within their individual functions; immersion in the details of those functions; and a willingness to collaboratively debate other functions during collective decision-making. When managers have these attributes, decisions are made in a coordinated fashion by the people most qualified to make them.

Deep expertise. Apple is not a company where general managers oversee managers; rather, it is a company where experts lead experts. The assumption is that it’s easier to train an expert to manage well than to train a manager to be an expert. At Apple, hardware experts manage hardware, software experts software, and so on. (Deviations from this principle are rare.) This approach cascades down all levels of the organization through areas of ever-increasing specialization. Apple’s leaders believe that world-class talent wants to work for and with other world-class talent in a specialty. It’s like joining a sports team where you get to learn from and play with the best.

Early on, Steve Jobs came to embrace the idea that managers at Apple should be experts in their area of management. In a 1984 interview he said, “We went through that stage in Apple where we went out and thought, Oh, we’re gonna be a big company, let’s hire professional management. We went out and hired a bunch of professional management. It didn’t work at all....They knew how to manage, but they didn’t know how to do anything. If you’re a great person, why do you want to work for somebody you can’t learn anything from? And you know what’s interesting? You know who the best managers are? They are the great individual contributors who never, ever want to be a manager but decide they have to be…because no one else is going to…do as good a job.”

One current example is Roger Rosner, who heads Apple’s software application business, which includes work-productivity apps such as Pages (word processing), Numbers (spreadsheets), and Keynote (presentations) along with GarageBand (music composition), iMovie (movie editing), and News (an app providing news content). Rosner, who studied electrical engineering at Carnegie Mellon, joined Apple in 2001 as a senior engineering manager and rose to become the director of iWork applications, the vice president of productivity apps, and since 2013 the VP of applications. With his deep expertise gained from previous experience as the director of engineering at several smaller software companies, Rosner exemplifies an expert leading experts.

In a functional organization, experts leading experts means that specialists create a deep bench in a given area, where they can learn from one another. For example, Apple’s more than 600 experts on camera hardware technology work in a group led by Graham Townsend, a camera expert. Because iPhones, iPads, laptops, and desktop computers all include cameras, these experts would be scattered across product lines if Apple were organized in business units. That would dilute their collective expertise, reducing their power to solve problems and generate and refine innovations.

Immersion in the details. One principle that permeates Apple is “Leaders should know the details of their organization three levels down,” because that is essential for speedy and effective cross-functional decision-making at the highest levels. If managers attend a decision-making meeting without the details at their disposal, the decision must either be made without the details or postponed. Managers tell war stories about making presentations to senior leaders who drill down into cells on a spreadsheet, lines of code, or a test result on a product.

Of course, the leaders of many companies insist that they and their teams are steeped in the details. But few organizations match Apple. Consider how its senior leaders pay extreme attention to the exact shape of products’ rounded corners. The standard method for rounding corners is to use an arc of a circle to connect the perpendicular sides of a rectangular object, which produces a somewhat abrupt transition from straight to curve. In contrast, Apple’s leaders insist on continuous curves, resulting in a shape known in the design community as a “squircle”: The slope starts sooner but is less abrupt. (See the exhibit “One Example of Apple’s Attention to Detail.”) An advantage of hardware products without abrupt changes in curvature is that they produce softer highlights (that is, little to no jump in light reflection along the corner). The difference is subtle, and executing on it isn’t simply a matter of a more complicated mathematical formula. It demands that Apple’s operations leaders commit to extremely precise manufacturing tolerances to produce millions of iPhones and other products with squircles. This deep immersion in detail isn’t just a concern that is pushed down to lower-level people; it is central at the leadership level.

Having leaders who are experts in their areas and can go deep into the details has profound implications for how Apple is run. Leaders can push, probe, and “smell” an issue. They know which details are important and where to focus their attention. Many people at Apple see it as liberating, even exhilarating, to work for experts, who provide better guidance and mentoring than a general manager would. Together, all can strive to do the best work of their lives in their chosen area.

Willingness to collaboratively debate. Apple has hundreds of specialist teams across the company, dozens of which may be needed for even one key component of a new product offering. For example, the dual-lens camera with portrait mode required the collaboration of no fewer than 40 specialist teams: silicon design, camera software, reliability engineering, motion sensor hardware, video engineering, core motion, and camera sensor design, to name just a few. How on earth does Apple Inc. develop and ship products that require such coordination? The answer is collaborative debate. Because no function is responsible for a product or a service on its own, cross-functional collaboration is crucial. When debates reach an impasse, as some inevitably do, higher-level managers weigh in as tiebreakers, including at times the CEO and the senior VPs. To do this at speed with sufficient attention to detail is challenging for even the best of leaders, making it all the more important that the company fill many senior positions from within the ranks of its VPs, who have experience in Apple’s way of operating.

However, given Apple’s size and scope, even the executive team can resolve only a limited number of stalemates. The many horizontal dependencies mean that ineffective peer relationships at the VP and director levels have the potential to undermine not only particular projects but the entire company. Consequently, for people to attain and remain in a leadership position within a function, they must be highly effective collaborators. That doesn’t mean people can’t express their points of view. Leaders are expected to hold strong, well-grounded views and advocate forcefully for them, yet also be willing to change their minds when presented with evidence that others’ views are better. Doing so is not always easy, of course. A leader’s ability to be both partisan and open-minded is facilitated by two things: deep understanding of and devotion to the company’s values and common purpose, and a commitment to separating how right from how hard a particular path is so that the difficulty of executing a decision doesn’t prevent its being selected.

The development of the iPhone’s portrait mode illustrates a fanatical attention to detail at the leadership level, intense collaborative debate among teams, and the power of a shared purpose to shape and ultimately resolve debates. In 2009 Hubel had the idea of developing an iPhone feature that would allow people to take portrait photos with bokeh—a Japanese term that refers to the pleasing blurring of a background—which photography experts generally consider to be of the highest quality. At that time only expensive single-lens reflex cameras could take such photos, but Hubel thought that with a dual-lens design and advanced,
Computational-photography techniques, Apple Inc. could add the capability in the iPhone. His idea aligned well with the camera team’s stated purpose: “More people taking better images more of the time.”

As the team worked to turn this idea into reality, several challenges emerged. The first attempts produced some amazing portrait pictures but also a number of “failure cases” in which the algorithm was unable to distinguish between the central object in sharp relief (a face, for instance) and the background being blurred. For example, if a person’s face was to be photographed from behind chicken wire, it was not possible to construct an algorithm that would capture the chicken wire to the side of the face with the same sharpness as the chicken wire in front of it. The wire to the side would be as blurred as the background.

One might say, “Who cares about the chicken wire case? That’s exceedingly rare.” But for the team, sidestepping rare or extreme situations—what engineers call corner cases—would violate Apple’s strict engineering standard of zero “artifacts,” meaning “any undesired or unintended alteration in data introduced in a digital process by an involved technique and/or technology.” Corner cases sparked “many tough discussions” between the camera team and other teams involved, recalls Myra Haggerty, the VP of sensor software and UX prototyping, who oversaw the firmware and algorithm teams. Sebastien Marineau-Mes, the VP to whom the camera software team ultimately reported, decided to defer the release of the feature until the following year to give the team time to better address failure cases—“a hard pill to swallow,” Hubel admits.

To get some agreement on quality standards, the engineering teams invited senior design and marketing leaders to meet, figuring that they would offer a new perspective. The design leaders brought an additional artistic sensibility to the debate, asking, “What makes a beautiful portrait?” To help reassess the zero-artifacts standard, they collected images from great portrait photographers. They noted, among other things, that these photos often had blurring at the edges of a face but sharpness on the eyes. So they charged the algorithm teams with achieving the same effect. When the teams succeeded, they knew they had an acceptable standard.

Another issue that emerged was the ability to preview a portrait photo with a blurred background. The camera team had designed the feature so that users could see its effect on their photos only after they had been taken, but the human interface (HI) design team pushed back, insisting that users should be able to see a “live preview” and get some guidance about how to make adjustments before taking the photo. Johnnie Manzari, a member of the HI team, gave the camera team a demo. “When we saw the demo, we realized that this is what we needed to do,” Townsend told us. The members of his camera hardware team weren’t sure they could do it, but difficulty was not an acceptable excuse for failing to deliver what would clearly be a superior user experience. After months of engineering effort, a key stakeholder, the video engineering team (responsible for the low-level software that controls sensor and camera operations) found a way, and the collaboration paid off. Portrait mode was central to Apple’s marketing of the iPhone 7 Plus. It proved a major reason for users’ choosing to buy and delighting in the use of the phone.

As this example shows, Apple’s collaborative debate involves people from various functions who disagree, push back, promote or reject ideas, and build on one another’s ideas to come up with the best solutions. It requires open-mindedness from senior leaders. It also requires those leaders to inspire, prod, or influence colleagues in other areas to contribute toward achieving their goals.

While Townsend is accountable for how great the camera is, he needed dozens of other teams—each of which had a long list of its own commitments—to contribute their time and effort to the portrait mode project. At Apple that’s known as accountability without control: You’re accountable for making the project succeed even though you don’t control all the other teams. This process can be messy yet produce great results. “Good mess” happens when various teams work with a shared purpose, as in the case of the portrait mode project. “Bad mess” occurs when teams push their own agendas ahead of common goals. Those who become associated with bad mess and don’t or can’t change their behavior are removed from leadership positions, if not from Apple altogether.


37. leadership at scale-


Apple Inc. Apple’s way of organizing has led to tremendous innovation and success over the past two decades. Yet it has not been without challenges, especially with revenues and head count having exploded since 2008. As the company has grown, entering new markets and moving into new technologies, its functional structure and leadership model have had to evolve. Deciding how to organize areas of expertise to best enable collaboration and rapid decision-making has been an important responsibility of the CEO. The adjustments Tim Cook has implemented in recent years include dividing the hardware function into hardware engineering and hardware technologies; adding artificial intelligence and machine learning as a functional area; and moving human interface out of software to merge it with industrial design, creating an integrated design function.

Another challenge posed by organizational growth is the pressure it imposes on the several hundred VPs and directors below the executive team. If Apple were to cap the size or scope of a senior leader’s organization to limit the number and breadth of details that the leader is expected to own, the company would need to hugely expand the number of senior leaders, making the kind of collaboration that has worked so well impossible to preserve. Cognizant of this problem, Apple has been quite disciplined about limiting the number of senior positions to minimize how many leaders must be involved in any cross-functional activity. In 2006, the year before the iPhone’s launch, the company had some 17,000 employees; by 2019 that number had grown more than eightfold, to 137,000. Meanwhile, the number of VPs approximately doubled, from 50 to 96. The inevitable result is that senior leaders head larger and more diverse teams of experts, meaning more details to oversee and new areas of responsibility that fall outside their core expertise.

In response, many Apple managers over the past five years or so have been evolving the leadership approach described above: experts leading experts, immersion in the details, and collaborative debate. We have codified these adaptions in what we call the discretionary leadership model, which we have incorporated into a new educational program for Apple’s VPs and directors. Its purpose is to address the challenge of getting this leadership approach to drive innovation in all areas of the company, not just product development, at an ever-greater scale. When Apple was smaller, it may have been reasonable to expect leaders to be experts on and immersed in the details of pretty much everything going on in their organizations. However, they now need to exercise greater discretion regarding where and how they spend their time and efforts. They must decide which activities demand their full attention to detail because those activities create the most value for Apple. Some of those will fall within their existing core expertise (what they still need to own), and some will require them to learn new areas of expertise. Activities that require less attention from the leader can be pushed down to others (and the leaders will either teach others or delegate in cases where they aren’t experts).

Rosner, the VP of applications, provides a good example. Like many other Apple managers, he has had to contend with three challenges arising from Apple’s tremendous growth. First, the size of his function has exploded over the past decade in terms of both head count (from 150 to about 1,000) and the number of projects under way at any given time. Clearly, he cannot dive into all the details of all those projects. Second, the scope of his portfolio has widened: Over the past 10 years he has assumed responsibility for new applications, including News, Clips (video editing), Books, and Final Cut Pro (advanced video editing). Although apps are his core area of expertise, some aspects of these—among them editorial content for News, how book publishing works, and video editing—involve matters in which Rosner is not an expert. Finally, as Apple’s product portfolio and number of projects have expanded, even more coordination with other functions is required, increasing the complexity of collaborating across the many units. For instance, whereas Rosner is responsible for the engineering side of News, other managers oversee the operating system on which it depends, the content, and the business relationships with content creators (such as the New York Times) and advertisers.

To cope, Rosner has adapted his role. As an expert who leads other experts, he had been immersed in details—especially those concerning the top-level aspects of software applications and their architecture that affect how users engage with the software. He also collaborated with managers across the company in projects that involved those areas. But with the expansion of his responsibilities, he has moved some things from his owning box—including traditional productivity apps such as Keynote and Pages—into his teaching box. (See the exhibit “Roger Rosner’s Discretionary Leadership.”) Now he guides and gives feedback to other team members so that they can develop software applications according to Apple’s norms. Being a teacher doesn’t mean that Rosner gives instruction at a whiteboard; rather, he offers strong, often passionate critiques of his team’s work. (Clearly, general managers without his core expertise would find it difficult to teach what they don’t know.)

The second challenge for Rosner involved the addition of activities beyond his original expertise. Six years ago he was given responsibility for the engineering and design of News. Consequently, he had to learn about publishing news content via an app—to understand news publications, digital advertising, machine learning to personalize news content, architecting for privacy, and how to incentivize publishers. Thus some of his work fell into the learning box. Here managers face a steep learning curve to acquire new skills. Given how demanding this is, only critical new activities should fall into this category. Over six years of intense learning, Rosner has mastered some of these areas, which are now in his owning box. As long as a particular activity remains in the learning box, leaders must adopt a beginner’s mindset, questioning subordinates in a way that suggests they don’t already know the answer (because they don’t). This differs starkly from the way leaders question subordinates about activities in the owning and teaching boxes.

Finally, Rosner has delegated some areas—including iMovie and GarageBand, in which he is not an expert—to people with the requisite capabilities. For activities in the delegating box, he assembles teams, agrees on objectives, monitors and reviews progress, and holds the teams accountable: the stuff of general management. Whereas Apple’s VPs spend most of their time in the owning and learning boxes, general managers at other companies tend to spend most of their time in the delegating box. Rosner estimates that he spends about 40% of his time on activities he owns (including collaboration with others in a given area), about 30% on learning, about 15% on teaching, and about 15% on delegating. These numbers vary by manager, of course, depending on their business and the needs at a given time.

The discretionary leadership model preserves the fundamental principle of an effective functional organization at scale—aligning expertise and decision rights. Apple can effectively move into new areas when leaders like Rosner take on new responsibilities outside their original expertise, and teams can grow in size when leaders teach others their craft and delegate work. We believe that Apple will continue to innovate and prosper by being organized this way.

APPLE’S FUNCTIONAL ORGANIZATION is rare, if not unique, among very large companies. It flies in the face of prevailing management theory that companies should be reorganized into divisions and business units as they become large. But something vital gets lost in a shift to business units: the alignment of decision rights with expertise. Why do companies so often cling to having general managers in charge of business units? One reason, we believe, is that making the change is difficult. It entails overcoming inertia, reallocating power among managers, changing an individual-oriented incentive system, and learning new ways of collaborating. That is daunting when a company already faces huge external challenges. An intermediate step may be to cultivate the experts-leading-experts model even within a business unit structure. For example, when filling the next senior management role, pick someone with deep expertise in that area as opposed to someone who might make the best general manager. But a full-fledged transformation requires that leaders also transition to a functional organization. Apple’s track record proves that the rewards may justify the risks. Its approach can produce extraordinary results.

39. More about founder-


Steve Jobs (Steven Paul Jobs) (February 24, 1955 – October 5, 2011) was an American businessman, inventor, and investor. A pioneer of the personal computer revolution of the 1970s and 1980s, Jobs co-founded Apple Inc. (as Apple Computer Company) with his early business partner Steve Wozniak in 1976. After the company's board of directors fired him in 1985, he founded NeXT the same year and purchased Pixar in 1986, becoming its chairman and majority shareholder until 2007. Jobs returned to Apple in 1997 as CEO, where he was closely involved with the creation and promotion of many of the company's most influential products until his resignation in 2011.

Jobs was born in San Francisco in 1955 and adopted shortly afterwards. He attended Reed College in 1972 before withdrawing that same year. In 1974, he traveled through India, seeking enlightenment before later studying Zen Buddhism. He and Wozniak co-founded Apple in 1976 to further develop and sell Wozniak's Apple I personal computer. Together, the duo gained fame and wealth a year later with production and sale of the Apple II, one of the first highly successful mass-produced microcomputers.

Jobs saw the commercial potential of the Xerox Alto in 1979, which was mouse-driven and had a graphical user interface (GUI). This led to the development of the largely unsuccessful Apple Lisa in 1983, followed by the breakthrough Macintosh in 1984, the first mass-produced computer with a GUI. The Macintosh launched the desktop publishing industry in 1985 (for example, the Aldus PageMaker) with the addition of the Apple LaserWriter, the first laser printer to feature vector graphics and PostScript.

In 1985, Jobs departed Apple after a long power struggle with the company's board and its then-CEO, John Sculley. That same year, Jobs took some Apple employees with him to found NeXT, a computer platform development company that specialized in computers for higher-education and business markets, serving as its CEO. In 1986, he bought the computer graphics division of Lucasfilm, which was spun off independently as Pixar. Pixar produced the first computer-animated feature film, Toy Story, and became a leading animation studio, producing dozens of commercially successful and critically acclaimed films.

In 1997, Jobs returned to Apple as CEO after the company's acquisition of NeXT. He was largely responsible for reviving Apple, which was on the verge of bankruptcy. He worked closely with British designer Jony Ive to develop a line of products and services that had larger cultural ramifications, beginning with the "Think different" advertising campaign, and leading to the iMac, iTunes, Mac OS X, Apple Store, iPod, iTunes Store, iPhone, App Store, and iPad. Jobs was also a board member at Gap Inc. from 1999 to 2002.

In 2003, Jobs was diagnosed with a pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor. He died of tumor-related respiratory arrest in 2011; in 2022, he was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom. Since his death, he has won 141 patents; Jobs holds over 450 patents in total.



40. Pre Apple 1974–1985



In February 1974, Steve Jobs returned to his parents' home in Los Altos and began looking for a job. He was soon hired by Atari, Inc. in Los Gatos as a computer technician. Back in 1973, Steve Wozniak designed his own version of the classic video game Pong and gave its electronics board to Jobs. According to Wozniak, Atari only hired Jobs because he took the board down to the company, and they thought that he had built it himself. Atari's cofounder Nolan Bushnell later described him as "difficult but valuable", pointing out that "he was very often the smartest guy in the room, and he would let people know that".

Jobs traveled to India in mid-1974 to visit Neem Karoli Baba at his Kainchi ashram with his Reed College friend and eventual Apple employee Daniel Kottke, searching for spiritual teachings. When they got to the Neem Karoli ashram, it was almost deserted because Neem Karoli Baba had died in September 1973. Then, they made a long trek up a dry riverbed to an ashram of Haidakhan Babaji.

After seven months, Jobs left India and returned to the US ahead of Daniel Kottke. Jobs had changed his appearance; his head was shaved, and he wore traditional Indian clothing. During this time, Jobs experimented with psychedelics, later calling his LSD experiences "one of the two or three most important things he had done in his life". He spent a period at the All One Farm, a commune in Oregon that was owned by Robert Friedland.

During this time period, Jobs and Chrisann Brennan both became practitioners of Zen Buddhism through the Zen master Kōbun Chino Otogawa. Jobs engaged in lengthy meditation retreats at the Tassajara Zen Mountain Center, the oldest Sōtō Zen monastery in the US. He considered taking up monastic residence at Eihei-ji in Japan, and maintained a lifelong appreciation for Zen, Japanese cuisine, and artists such as Hasui Kawase.

Jobs returned to Atari in early 1975, and that summer, Bushnell assigned him to create a circuit board for the arcade video game Breakout in as few chips as possible, knowing that Jobs would recruit Wozniak for help. During his day job at Hewlett-Packard, Wozniak drew sketches of the circuit design; at night, he joined Jobs at Atari and continued to refine the design, which Jobs implemented on a breadboard. According to Bushnell, Atari offered $100 (equivalent to about $600 in 2025) for each TTL chip that was eliminated in the machine. Jobs made a deal with Wozniak to split the fee evenly between them if Wozniak could minimize the number of chips. Much to the amazement of Atari engineers, within four days Wozniak reduced the TTL count to 45, far below the usual 100, though Atari later re-engineered it to make it easier to test and add a few missing features. According to Wozniak, Jobs told him that Atari paid them only $750 (instead of the actual $5,000), and that Wozniak's share was thus $375. Wozniak did not learn about the actual bonus until ten years later but said that if Jobs had told him about it and explained that he needed the money, Wozniak would have given it to him.

Jobs and Wozniak attended meetings of the Homebrew Computer Club in 1975, which was a stepping stone to the development and marketing of the first Apple computer. According to a document released by the United States Department of Defense, Jobs claimed that in 1975, he was arrested in Eugene after being questioned for being a minor in possession of alcohol. Jobs alleged that he "didn't have any alcohol", but police questioned him, and subsequently determined that he had an outstanding arrest warrant for an unpaid speeding ticket. Jobs claimed he then paid the $50 fine. The arrest allegedly occurred "behind a store".



41. Apple (1976–1985)



By March 1976, Steve Wozniak completed the basic design of the Apple I computer and showed it to Steve Jobs, who suggested that they sell it; Wozniak was at first skeptical of the idea but later agreed. In April of that same year, Jobs, Wozniak, and administrative overseer Ronald Wayne founded Apple Inc. (then Apple Computer Company) as a business partnership in Jobs's parents' Crist Drive home on April 1, 1976. The operation originally started in Jobs's bedroom and later moved to the garage. Wayne stayed briefly, leaving Jobs and Wozniak as the active primary cofounders of the company.

The two decided on the name "Apple" after Jobs returned from the All One Farm in Oregon and told Wozniak about his time in the farm's apple orchard. Jobs originally planned to produce bare printed circuit boards of the Apple I and sell them to computer hobbyists for $50 each. To fund the first batch, Wozniak sold his HP scientific calculator and Jobs sold his Volkswagen van. Later that year, computer retailer Paul Terrell purchased 50 fully assembled Apple I units for $500 each. Eventually, about 200 Apple I computers were produced in total.

A neighbor on Crist Drive recalled Jobs as an odd individual who would greet his clients "with his underwear hanging out, barefoot and hippie-like". Another neighbor, Larry Waterland, who had just earned his PhD in chemical engineering at Stanford University, recalled dismissing Jobs's budding business compared to the established industry of giant mainframe computers with big decks of punch cards: "Steve took me over to the garage. He had a circuit board with a chip on it, a DuMont TV set, a Panasonic cassette tape deck and a keyboard. He said, 'This is an Apple computer.' I said, 'You've got to be joking.' I dismissed the whole idea." Jobs's friend from Reed College and India, Daniel Kottke, recalled that as an early Apple employee, "he was the only person who worked in the garage ... Woz would show up once a week with his latest code. Steve Jobs didn't get his hands dirty in that sense." Kottke also stated that much of the early work took place in Jobs's kitchen, where he spent hours on the phone trying to find investors for the company.

They received funding from semi-retired Intel product marketing manager and engineer Mike Markkula. Scott McNealy said that Jobs broke a "glass age ceiling" in Silicon Valley because he'd created a very successful company at a young age. Markkula brought Apple to the attention of Arthur Rock, who, after looking at the crowded Apple booth at the Home Brew Computer Show, started with a $60,000 investment and went on the Apple board. Jobs was not pleased when Markkula recruited Mike Scott from National Semiconductor in February 1977 to serve as the first president and CEO of Apple.

For what characterizes Apple is that its scientific staff always acted and performed like artists—in a field filled with dry personalities limited by the rational and binary worlds they inhabit, Apple's engineering teams had passion. They always believed that what they were doing was important and, most of all, fun. Working at Apple was never just a job; it was also a crusade, a mission, to bring better computer power to people. At its roots, that attitude came from Steve Jobs. It was "Power to the People", the slogan of the sixties, rewritten in technology for the eighties and called Macintosh.

After Chrisann Brennan returned from her own journey to India, she and Jobs fell in love again, as Brennan noted changes in him that she attributes to Kōbun Chino Otogawa. It was also at this time that Jobs displayed a prototype Apple II computer to Brennan and his parents in their living room. Brennan notes a shift in this time period, where the two main influences on Jobs were Apple Inc. and Kobun.

In April 1977, Jobs and Wozniak introduced the Apple II at the West Coast Computer Faire. It is the first consumer product to have been sold by Apple Computer. Primarily designed by Wozniak, Jobs oversaw the development of its unusual case and Rod Holt developed the unique power supply. During the design stage, Jobs argued that the Apple II should have two expansion slots, while Wozniak wanted eight. After a heated argument, Wozniak threatened that Jobs should "go get himself another computer". They later agreed on eight slots. The Apple II became one of the first highly successful mass-produced microcomputer products in the world.

As Jobs became more successful with his new company, his relationship with Brennan grew more complex. In 1977, the success of Apple was now a part of their relationship, and Brennan, Daniel Kottke, and Jobs moved into a house near the Apple office in Cupertino. Brennan eventually took a position in the shipping department at Apple. Brennan's relationship with Jobs deteriorated as his position with Apple grew, and she began to consider ending the relationship. In October 1977, Brennan was approached by Rod Holt, who asked her to take "a paid apprenticeship designing blueprints for the Apples". Both Holt and Jobs believed that it would be a good position for her, given her artistic abilities. Holt was particularly eager that she take the position and puzzled by her ambivalence toward it.

Brennan's decision, however, was overshadowed by the fact that she realized she was pregnant, and that Jobs was the father. It took her a few days to tell Jobs, whose face, according to Brennan, "turned ugly" at the news. At the same time, according to Brennan, at the beginning of her third trimester, Jobs said to her: "I never wanted to ask that you get an abortion. I just didn't want to do that." He also refused to discuss the pregnancy with her. Brennan turned down the internship and decided to leave Apple. A few weeks before she was due to give birth, Brennan was invited to deliver her baby at the All One Farm. She accepted the offer.

When Jobs was 23, Brennan gave birth to her baby, Lisa Brennan-Jobs, on May 17, 1978. Jobs went there for the birth after he was contacted by Robert Friedland, their mutual friend and the farm owner. While distant, Jobs worked with her on a name for the baby, which they discussed while sitting in the fields on a blanket. Brennan suggested the name "Lisa" which Jobs also liked and notes that Jobs was very attached to the name "Lisa" while he was also publicly denying paternity. She would discover later that during this time, Jobs was preparing to unveil a new kind of computer that he wanted to give a female name. He stated that he was using the phrase "Local Integrated Software Architecture" as an alternative explanation for the Apple Lisa. Decades later, however, Jobs admitted that it was named for his daughter.

When Jobs denied paternity, a DNA test established him as Lisa's father. It required him to pay Brennan monthly support in addition to returning the welfare money she had received. Jobs paid increased support when Apple went public and made him a millionaire. Later, Brennan agreed to an interview with Michael Moritz for Time magazine, released on January 3, 1983, in which she discussed her relationship with Jobs. Rather than name Jobs the Person of the Year, the magazine named the personal computer the "Machine of the Year". In the issue, Jobs questioned the reliability of the paternity test, while the magazine noted that both the baby girl and the machine shared the same name Lisa.

In 1978, at age 23, Jobs was worth over $1 million. By age 25, his net worth grew to an estimated $250 million. He was also one of the youngest people ever to make the Forbes list of the nation's richest people—and one of only a handful to have done it themselves, without inherited wealth. In 1982, Jobs bought an apartment in Manhattan at The San Remo. Although he never lived there, he spent years renovating it with I. M. Pei. In 1983, Jobs lured John Sculley away from Pepsi-Cola to serve as Apple's CEO.

In 1984, Jobs bought the Jackling House and resided there for a decade. Thereafter, he leased it out until 2000. In 2004, Jobs received permission from the town of Woodside to demolish the house to build a smaller one, and it was finally demolished in 2011.

Jobs took over development of the Macintosh in 1981 from early Apple employee Jef Raskin. Wozniak and Raskin had heavily influenced the early program, and Wozniak was on leave due to an airplane crash, making it easier for Jobs to take over. On January 22, 1984, Apple aired a Super Bowl commercial titled "1984". On January 24, 1984, Jobs introduced the Macintosh at Apple's annual shareholders meeting held in the Flint Auditorium at De Anza College. Macintosh engineer Andy Hertzfeld described the scene as "pandemonium". The Macintosh, inspired by the Lisa and Xerox PARC, was widely acclaimed by the media with strong initial sales, though its limitations led to a rapid decline later in 1984.

John Sculley and Jobs had differing visions for Apple. Sculley favored open architecture computers like the Apple II, while Jobs focused on the closed Macintosh system. By early 1985, the Macintosh's failure to defeat the IBM PC strengthened Sculley's position. In May 1985, Sculley proposed reorganizing Apple, removing Jobs from the Macintosh group. In response, Jobs planned to remove Sculley, but the plan was leaked. Confronted, Jobs said he would leave Apple. The board declined his resignation, but eventually, on September 17, 1985, Jobs officially resigned. Five senior Apple employees joined him in founding NeXT.

After Jobs left, the Macintosh struggled despite early success. Bill Gates and Microsoft pressured Apple for licensing agreements. Cheap IBM PC clones running Microsoft software began dominating the market. Competing graphical user interfaces such as Windows, IBM's TopView, and Digital Research's GEM spread widely. By the late 1980s, graphical interfaces became standard, reducing the Macintosh's advantage and making it clear that Apple could not compete alone against the IBM-compatible market.



42. Innovations and Designs

Steve Jobs’s design aesthetic was influenced by philosophies of Zen Buddhism and Buddhism. In India, he experienced Buddhism during his seven-month spiritual journey, and his sense of intuition was shaped by the spiritual people with whom he studied. Jobs gained insights regarding industrial design from Richard Sapper. According to Apple co-founder Steve Wozniak, “Steve didn’t ever code. He wasn’t an engineer and he didn’t do any original design.” Daniel Kottke, one of Apple’s earliest employees and a college friend of Jobs, stated: “Between Woz and Jobs, Woz was the innovator, the inventor. Steve Jobs was the marketing person.”

Jobs is listed as either primary inventor or co-inventor in 346 United States patents or patent applications related to a wide range of technologies, from computers and portable devices to user interfaces (including touch-based systems), speakers, keyboards, power adapters, and packaging designs. His contributions to most patents were related to “the look and feel of the product.” He and his industrial design chief Jony Ive are named on about 200 patents. Most of these are design patents rather than utility patents, focusing on specific product appearances such as the original and lamp-style iMacs and the PowerBook G4 Titanium. He holds 43 issued US patents on inventions. The patent for the Mac OS X Dock user interface with the “magnification” feature was issued the day before his death. Although Jobs had limited involvement in the engineering side of early Apple computers, he later used his CEO role to directly influence product design.

Throughout his career, Jobs worked closely with long-time marketing executive Joanna Hoffman, one of the few people at Apple and NeXT who could challenge him while maintaining a strong working relationship. Even during serious illness, Jobs continued designing, sketching new devices such as a hospital-compatible iPad holder. He also suggested improvements to medical device designs, including simplifying the oxygen monitor used in hospitals.

Apple I

The Apple I was designed entirely by Steve Wozniak, but Jobs had the idea of selling the computer, which led to the founding of Apple Inc. in 1976. Jobs and Wozniak constructed several prototypes by hand, funding production by selling personal belongings. Eventually, around 200 units were produced. A key innovation of the Apple I was its built-in video display circuitry, allowing it to connect to a standard monitor or television instead of requiring an expensive terminal.

Apple II

The Apple II is an 8-bit home computer and one of the first highly successful mass-produced microcomputers, designed primarily by Wozniak. Jobs oversaw the development of its distinctive outer case, while Rod Holt designed the power supply. It was introduced in 1977 at the West Coast Computer Faire and became Apple’s first major consumer product. The Apple II was first sold on June 10, 1977.

Lisa

The Lisa was a personal computer developed by Apple starting in 1978 and released in the early 1980s. It was the first personal computer with a graphical user interface. Although it sold poorly at around 100,000 units, it introduced advanced features later used in the Macintosh and other computers. After being removed from the Lisa project, Jobs took over the Macintosh project and later oversaw the transition of Lisa into the Macintosh XL.

Macintosh

The Macintosh was launched on January 24, 1984, as the first mass-market personal computer featuring an integrated graphical user interface and mouse. Jobs led its development after taking over from Jef Raskin. The launch included a famous Super Bowl advertisement directed by Ridley Scott, inspired by Nineteen Eighty-Four. Although the Macintosh was innovative, its high price limited its competitiveness against systems like the IBM Personal Computer and Commodore 64. Despite this, it became influential in education and desktop publishing.

NeXT Computer

After leaving Apple in 1985, Jobs founded NeXT and introduced the NeXT Computer in 1988. This system played a key role in technological history, as Tim Berners-Lee used it to create the first web browser. Its operating system, NeXTSTEP, later formed the foundation for Apple’s macOS and iOS.

iMac

The iMac G3, introduced in 1998 after Jobs returned to Apple, featured a bold, colorful design that stood out from traditional computers. Its translucent “Bondi Blue” casing and all-in-one design helped redefine consumer computing. The iMac eliminated the floppy disk drive and adopted USB connectivity, helping popularize USB devices across the industry.

iTunes

iTunes is a media player and digital library developed by Apple for managing music, videos, and other media. Through the iTunes Store, users could purchase and download songs, movies, podcasts, and more. It became a central platform connecting Apple devices such as the iPhone, iPad, and iPod.

iPod

The first iPod was released on October 23, 2001. Its compact size and storage capacity made it a breakthrough in portable music devices. The iPod’s success established Apple as a major player in the music industry and paved the way for future products like the iPhone.


iPhone

Apple began developing the iPhone in 2005, releasing the first model on June 29, 2007. It combined a phone, music player, and internet device into one touchscreen smartphone. The iPhone 3G (2008) added GPS and faster data, while later versions improved performance and features. The iPhone 4 introduced video calling and a refined design, and the iPhone 4s introduced Siri, a voice assistant.

iPad

The iPad, introduced in 2010, is a tablet computer based on a multi-touch interface. It includes Wi-Fi and optional cellular connectivity, and its design focuses on simplicity and portability. By April 2015, over 250 million iPads had been sold worldwide.

44. Stores


Apple Inc.’s retail presence, known as the Apple Store, has played a major role in shaping its relationship with customers. One of its most iconic locations is the Apple Fifth Avenue flagship store. Another notable location is the Apple Store Regent Street, where customers have visited the Genius Bar for technical support and assistance.

The first Apple Stores were opened in May 2001 by then-CEO Steve Jobs, after years of unsuccessful attempts at store-within-a-store concepts. Recognizing the need for better retail presentation, Jobs began revamping Apple’s retail strategy in 1997 to improve customer relationships and hired Ron Johnson in 2000 to lead the effort. Jobs also relaunched Apple’s online store in 1997 before opening the first physical locations in 2001. Initially, media critics predicted failure, but the stores quickly outperformed competitors and, within three years, generated $1 billion in annual sales—becoming the fastest retailer in history to reach that milestone.

Over time, Apple expanded its retail footprint significantly. By December 2017, the company operated 499 stores across 22 countries. Strong product sales placed Apple among the top-tier global retailers, with over $16 billion in sales in 2011 alone.

Apple Stores underwent a major redesign starting in May 2016. This transformation included more open spaces, redesigned interiors, and rebranded sections aimed at improving interaction between customers and Apple professionals.

Many Apple Stores are located within shopping malls, but Apple has also built standalone flagship stores in prominent locations worldwide. These stores are known for their distinctive architectural designs, including the use of glass staircases and glass cube structures. Apple has received design patents and architectural awards for these innovations.

The success of Apple Stores has influenced the broader retail industry, particularly consumer electronics stores, many of which have struggled due to Apple’s higher perceived quality of service and product presentation.

Due to the brand’s popularity, Apple receives a large number of job applications, especially from younger workers. Employees typically receive above-average pay, educational support, healthcare benefits, and product discounts. However, there are limited opportunities for long-term career advancement within Apple’s retail division.



45. Privacy-



Apple Inc. has publicly taken a strong pro-privacy stance, making privacy-focused features a key part of its conferences, marketing, and overall brand image. With the release of iOS 8 in 2014, Apple began encrypting all data on iOS devices using user passcodes, making it difficult for the company to provide customer data even when requested by law enforcement.

As cloud storage became more common, Apple introduced new privacy-preserving techniques in 2016. These included performing deep learning scans for facial recognition directly on users’ devices and encrypting data before uploading it to iCloud. The company also introduced the concept of differential privacy, a method of collecting aggregated data from many users while preserving individual anonymity.

Despite these measures, Apple has assisted law enforcement in criminal investigations by providing iCloud backups when legally required. At the same time, its push toward biometric authentication in newer iPhone models has raised concerns, as such methods may not offer the same legal protections as passcodes in certain jurisdictions.

With the release of iOS 14, Apple introduced “App Tracking Transparency,” requiring developers to request user permission before tracking data. This feature faced criticism from Facebook, whose advertising model relies heavily on user data tracking. However, adoption rates remained low, with only a small percentage of users opting into tracking globally.

Before launching iOS 15, Apple announced additional measures to combat child exploitation. These included parental alerts for sensitive content in iMessage and on-device hashing of images uploaded to iCloud to detect known abusive material. While supported by law enforcement and child protection advocates, these features were criticized by privacy groups such as the Electronic Frontier Foundation, which warned of potential misuse and risks to user privacy.

Regulatory scrutiny has also been significant. Ireland Data Protection Commission launched an investigation into whether Apple complied with GDPR rules regarding personal data and targeted advertising practices.

In December 2019, security researcher Brian Krebs reported that the iPhone 11 Pro displayed a location services indicator even when individual location settings were disabled. Apple clarified that this behavior was related to ultra-wideband technology requirements and that location data processing occurred entirely on the device without being transmitted externally. The company later added more transparency features, allowing users to see which services were using location data.

According to a 2022 report by Bloomberg News, Apple had provided certain user data—such as phone numbers, addresses, and IP information—to individuals posing as law enforcement using forged requests. In response, Apple stated that it reviews all such requests for legal validity and uses advanced systems to detect fraudulent or abusive data requests.




45.Taxes

Apple Inc. has established subsidiaries in low-tax jurisdictions such as Ireland, Netherlands, Luxembourg, and the British Virgin Islands to reduce the amount of taxes it pays globally. According to The New York Times, Apple was among the first technology companies in the 1980s to structure its international sales operations so that revenue could be recorded in low-tax regions, thereby avoiding higher taxes in other countries. During the late 1980s, Apple also became a pioneer of the accounting strategy known as the “Double Irish with a Dutch sandwich,” which reduced taxes by routing profits through Irish subsidiaries, then through the Netherlands, and finally to Caribbean tax havens.

In October 2012, Charlie Elphicke published research showing that several multinational corporations, including Apple, generated billions in profits in the United Kingdom while paying an effective tax rate of only about 3 percent—far below standard corporate tax rates. He urged George Osborne to require such companies, including Google and The Coca-Cola Company, to disclose their effective tax rates and suggested withholding government contracts from companies that did not pay what he considered a fair share of taxes.

A 2013 report by the United States Senate found that Apple had held billions of dollars in profits within Irish subsidiaries, allowing it to pay little or no taxes to any government by exploiting differences between Irish and US tax residency rules. One major subsidiary, which managed Apple’s retail operations across Europe, reportedly paid no corporate income tax for five years. On May 21, 2013, CEO Tim Cook defended the company’s tax strategies during a Senate hearing.

Apple has stated that it is the largest taxpayer in the United States, with an effective tax rate of about 26% as of Q2 FY2016. In a 2017 interview, Tim Cook also claimed that Apple was the largest taxpayer globally.

In 2016, following a two-year investigation, the European Commission ruled that Apple’s tax arrangement in Ireland constituted illegal state aid and ordered the company to pay €13 billion in back taxes—the largest corporate tax fine ever imposed. However, this decision was later overturned by the European General Court, which found that there was insufficient evidence to support the ruling.

In 2018, Apple repatriated $285 billion to the United States, resulting in a tax payment of approximately $38 billion spread over eight years.




46. Energy and Resources

In 2010, Climate Counts, a group focused on guiding consumers toward environmentally responsible companies, gave Apple Inc. a score of 52 out of 100, placing it in the “Striding” category. This marked a significant improvement from May 2008, when Apple received only 11 out of 100 and ranked last among electronics companies, with Climate Counts labeling it as a company to avoid for climate-conscious consumers.

Following a protest by Greenpeace, Apple announced on April 17, 2012, that it would end its reliance on coal and transition to 100% renewable energy. By 2013, Apple was powering all of its data centers with renewable energy, and overall, 75% of the company’s energy usage came from clean sources.

In May 2015, Greenpeace evaluated the environmental impact of major technology companies and praised Apple’s efforts, stating that its commitment to renewable energy had set a new standard for the industry and demonstrated that a fully renewable-powered internet was achievable.

By 2016, Apple reported that 100% of its operations in the United States and all its data centers were powered by renewable energy, while 93% of its global operations relied on renewable sources. However, since facilities remain connected to local power grids that include fossil fuels, Apple uses carbon offsets to account for its electricity consumption.

The Electronic Product Environmental Assessment Tool evaluates the environmental impact of electronic products, assigning Gold, Silver, or Bronze ratings based on sustainability and efficiency. All Apple tablets, notebooks, desktops, and displays assessed by EPEAT have received the highest “Gold” rating. Although Apple’s data centers recycle water multiple times, increased operations led to total water usage reaching 573 million US gallons (2.2 million cubic meters) in 2015.

On March 21, 2016, Apple provided an update on its environmental initiatives, reaffirming its goal of achieving 100% renewable energy worldwide. Lisa P. Jackson, the company’s Vice President of Environment, Policy and Social Initiatives, announced that 93% of Apple’s global operations were already powered by renewable energy. Apple also emphasized its sustainable packaging efforts, stating that 99% of paper used in packaging came from recycled or responsibly managed sources.

On August 16, 2016, Apple announced that Lens Technology, one of its major suppliers in China, would transition to 100% renewable energy for Apple-related glass production by 2018. Additionally, all 14 of Apple’s final assembly sites in China achieved UL’s Zero Waste to Landfill certification, ensuring that manufacturing waste is reused, recycled, composted, or converted into energy. This initiative diverted nearly 140,000 metric tons of waste from landfills.

On July 21, 2020, Apple announced its goal to become carbon neutral across its entire business, manufacturing supply chain, and product life cycle by 2030. This plan includes reducing emissions through innovations in product design, energy efficiency, renewable energy adoption, material usage, and carbon removal technologies.

In June 2024, the United States Environmental Protection Agency published a report regarding a computer manufacturing facility leased by Apple in 2015 in Santa Clara, known internally as “Aria.” The report indicated that Apple may have violated federal regulations under the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act. According to Bloomberg, the facility was used for developing microLED display technology.

The EPA’s inspection, conducted in August 2023 following a tip from a former Apple employee, found that Apple may have improperly handled waste under state regulations and miscalculated the effectiveness of its activated carbon filtration systems, which are used to remove volatile organic compounds from the air.
